# One in five households in Kosova cannot keep their homes adequately warm

That is Kosova's own official figure for 2024 — 21.6% of households, from ASK's
Survey on Income and Living Conditions. It is the EU's standard energy-poverty
indicator, and Kosova's series tells a more complicated story than a single
number suggests.

In 2018 — the one year Kosova's data was transmitted to and validated by
Eurostat — the figure was 40.2%, among the highest in Europe (the EU-27 average
was 7.6%). Among households below the poverty line, it was 53.1%. By ASK's
published series, the rate has since roughly halved. Taken at face value, that
is a substantial improvement.

There are reasons to hold that trend loosely. The 2019 value (62.6%) is a clear
outlier with no published explanation, the indicator is self-reported, and only
the 2018 point has ever been externally validated. An apparent improvement in a
self-reported affordability measure can reflect real welfare gains — or changes
in survey practice. With one validated data point, the honest answer is that we
cannot fully distinguish the two.

Regionally, the dispersion is wide: from 8.8% in the EU-27 and 9.3% in Serbia
to 33.8% in Albania. Kosova's 2024 figure sits mid-range — better than Albania
and North Macedonia, worse than Montenegro, Serbia and the EU average — though
unlike its neighbours' figures, it is not Eurostat-validated. Bosnia is absent
from the comparison entirely: it does not report the indicator at all.

The price backdrop has not been easing: electricity, gas and other fuels were
up 14.3% year-on-year as of March 2026.

What the public data cannot say is who cannot keep warm. The only
distributional cut that has ever existed is the 2018 poverty split. ASK's
Household Budget Survey publishes no expenditure breakdowns by income, ends in
2022, and does not separate energy from housing costs. And the indicator itself
conflates three things — income, prices, and the thermal quality of housing —
that can only be separated with household-level data. Until that exists, energy
poverty in Kosova can be counted, roughly, but not explained.
